# From transmitter readings to a commissioning decision: pump J100 in a carbon-capture pilot plant

**Draft — ~600 words**

## The problem

Bringing a CO₂ capture pilot plant up from cold to steady state is one of the
least glamorous and most consequential parts of any carbon-capture programme.
Long before you can quote a regeneration energy or a CO₂ removal efficiency,
you have to answer a much more basic question: *how fast can we run the lean
amine pump without tripping a level alarm or starving the absorber?* On the
Imperial College MEA rig, that pump is **J100** — a small centrifugal pump
that circulates 15 % MEA between the absorber sump and the stripper feed. Push
it too slowly and the column dries out; push it too hard and LT101 alarms on
low stripper level. The commissioning engineer's job is to find the safe
operating envelope before the rig is handed over for actual experiments.

## How MEA capture works, in one paragraph

Post-combustion MEA capture is a continuous absorption-stripping cycle. Flue
gas enters the bottom of a packed absorber and rises counter-current to a lean
aqueous monoethanolamine solvent trickling down the packing. CO₂ reacts with
the amine to form a carbamate, leaving a "rich" solvent at the column base.
That rich stream is preheated against returning lean solvent in a cross
exchanger, fed to a stripper, and regenerated by a steam-driven reboiler at
~120 °C. The recovered CO₂ is dried and compressed; the regenerated lean
solvent is cooled and recycled back to the absorber. The whole process lives
or dies on the **stable circulation** of solvent — which is exactly where pump
commissioning earns its keep.

## What `meapy` lets you calculate, and why it matters

The `meapy.pump` module turns a fistful of commissioning data points into an
auditable safe-speed recommendation. You hand it (i) the pump speed
set-points you tested, (ii) the steady-state MEA level recorded at LT101 for
each, and (iii) the flowrate observed at FT103. It fits an exponential model
to the level-vs-speed data (because draining is geometric, not linear), a
linear model to the flow-vs-speed data, then walks both models out to the
plant alarm thresholds and reports the *minimum* of the two as the binding
constraint. No spreadsheets, no eyeballing trendlines, and — critically — the
fit's R² travels with the recommendation, so a downstream reviewer can see
how confident the model is.

## The key call

```python
import numpy as np
from meapy.pump import (
    fit_exponential_level_model,
    fit_linear_flowrate_model,
    safe_pump_speed,
)

speeds = np.array([10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 53])      # %
levels = np.array([80.1, 65.3, 52.0, 38.7, 24.1, 15.0])  # % from LT101
flows  = np.array([177, 365, 553, 740, 927, 980])         # kg/h from FT103

level_model = fit_exponential_level_model(speeds, levels)
flow_model  = fit_linear_flowrate_model(speeds, flows)
result      = safe_pump_speed(level_model, flow_model)
print(result)
```

## Reading the output

The `PumpCommissioningResult` printout tells the engineer everything in one
place: the recommended safe speed in %, the MEA level and flowrate predicted
at that speed, the speed at which each alarm would trip, and — most
importantly — the *limiting constraint*. On the dataset above, the level
alarm is binding: the stripper would empty before the flowmeter saturates.
The recommendation is therefore "level-limited," and any subsequent change
that affects the stripper inventory (a different solvent loading, a higher
reboiler duty) should re-trigger the analysis. The R² printed by the fit
makes the model's confidence visible, so the operator knows whether to add an
extra margin.
